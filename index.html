Our Story

It’s 2010. My family is making our first pilgrimage to Mecca. Our guide, a student from Senegal, takes a moment to share with us the story of his father, Ibrahim Cisse.

Back in Senegal, Ibrahim has begun to adopt orphans, opening up his modest home and sharing his low income with them, simply to help make their lives a little better.

Talibe, the name for these abandoned 4-15 year old children, live on the streets, relying on begging for their survival.

Over the years, their number has only continued to grow – and since. These children have no one to care for them, they become easy targets for predators and often end up being physically abused, exploited and even murdered.

It’s 2015. I am vacationing with my family in Florida. In the dining room of the hotel, while waiting for our breakfast to arrive, I decide to check a Senegalese news websites on my phone.

The headline reads: “Big Fire Where Talibes Sleep”. Nine innocent children, aged 6-10 years old, lost their lives that day.

In that moment, my heart drops. I can’t hold back the tears when I see a photo of the thin piece of sponge they use for a mattress and the tattered cloth for a blanket.

A million thoughts race through my mind.

When was the last time these children had a decent meal? A drink of clean water? Or a bath? What about a glass of milk – something many talibes desperately need to combat their sickle cell anemia?

The truth is, they may have never had one. Ever. And they probably never got a hug, or heard the words, “I love you.” The thoughts keep coming. I just can’t stop them. I’ve never felt so helpless in my entire life.

Right then and there I know it is time for me to act.

Time to pursue what I have always believed is my mission on earth: To reach out and somehow make a real difference in the lives of poor children. To give them a healthy start in life, give them the opportunity to learn and, especially, protect them from harm.

I start to think about how to raise awareness around what is happening to these children and how to get people involved in improving the way the world treats poor children.

With the encouragement and support of a dear friend, Isabella, who has dedicated her own life to the fight for children’s rights in Kenya, as well as that of my close friend and colleague, Shivani Cancienne, I start the Mathurin Children’s Foundation.

First, I contact Mr. Cisse, the kind man who was already helping some of these children. We discuss the possibility of working together to make a significant differences in these children’s lives. I learn that a few years ago, with the support of the Senegalese government, Mr. Cisse was able to acquire an acre of land close to his house.

Our first line of work is to build safe and adequate shelter for the children. At the same time, we must provide for their most basic needs – food, clothing, and clean water and milk for them to drink.

Today, almost 500 children live on this land.

Thanks to the generosity of a few dedicated friends, we have begun making small contributions to fund much-needed purchases of food and clothing. But our goals – and their needs – are much bigger!

With your help we will succeed in creating a caring community to nurture and nourish these innocent children, and establish a safety net that will sustain them over time.

Board of Directors

Hawa Kader Gaye Dia: Founder and Executive Director

Hawa grew up in Senegal, West Africa, and moved to the USA in 1991 where she attended the University of the District of Columbia and earned her Bachelor’s Degree in Business Management. Hawa also completed advanced degree work in Education, Psychology and School Directorship. Her decades of experience as a teacher sparked a powerful and passionate vision to provide a better life for less fortunate children in Senegal, especially those born with Sickle Cell Anemia. She founded Mathurin Children’s Foundation to educate the world about the challenges these children face and to provide resources that can make a profound difference in their lives. The foundation is named for Hawa’s beloved cousin Mathurin who died of Sickle Cell Anemia at the age of five.



Shivani Cancienne: Director of Operations and Development

Shivani was born and raised in Northern California and she earned her bachelor’s degree in Education at Chaminade University in Honolulu. Her joy and love for teaching children has spanned over forty years and is still going strong. A skilled administrator, Shivani served as human resources manager for an international nonprofit and is currently Director of Growing Light Montessori School in Oakland, California. Shivani is also trained in mediation and conflict resolution with an emphasis on mindfulness. She dreamed of one day being part of an organization that helps children in need in Africa. After meeting Hawa and learning about her mission and foundation she now has the opportunity to see her dream come true.


Heyjin Lee: Administrative Coordinator

Heyjin Lee moved to the USA from Seoul, South Korea in 1994 and earned her bachelor’s degree in Education at the University of the Nations in Kona, HI. Her decades of teaching experience and her love for children drive her passion for charitable work. Heyjin is committed to make the world a better place by helping disadvantaged children improve their lives. She is very grateful for the opportunity to work with other wonderful people who share a common vision of health, education and opportunity for the children of Senegal.
